## Cables & Connectors
	## Network Cables
		### Description
			A medium through which data is transferred from one device to another.
			### There are Three primary types of network cables
		### Copper Medium
			The most common medium used in both coaxial and twisted pair cables.
		### Cat
			Categories, also referred to as Cat, describes the performance of twisted pair cables.
			All Cat cables 2 through 8 are composed of four pairs of twisted-pairs totaling eight individual cables in one cable sheath.
			Cat 5
				100 Mbps
				100 meter max distance
			Cat 5e
				1 Gbps
				100 meter max distance
				Increased speed due to the separated twisted pairs and increased number of twists.
			Cat 6
				10 Gbps
				55 meters max distance
			Cat 6a
				10 Gbps
				100 meters max distance
		### Coaxial
			### What is a coaxial cable?
				A single copper-cored cable contained in an inner insulation layer.
				Then it is surrounded by a wire mesh conductor.
				Finally it is enclosed in an outer insulation layer.
			Where are Coaxial cables used?
				Mostly used in cable and satellite devices.
				Not as commonly used in modern networks.
			What specifies the Coaxial Cables?
				Radio Guide (RG) system
			What two cables do you need to know?
				RG-59
					Thinner gauge cable
					low bandwidth and low frequency
					Used in cable TV
				RG-6
					Heavier gauge
					High-bandwidth and high-frequency applications
					Used in satellites and cable modems
		### Twisted Copper Pair
			What is a twisted copper pair?
						Individual wires twisted into pairs and then twisted with other pairs
			Are Ethernet cables twisted pair?
				YES
			Twisted Copper Pair Types#card
				Shielded twisted pair (STP)
				Unshielded twisted pair (UTP)
				Typically in configurations of two or four
			Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP)
				A cable that doesn't have any foil shielding around it.
				Doesn't protect against EMI
			Shielded Twisted Pair (STP)
				A cable surrounded by a braided foil sheathing which prevents EMI
				Used in Cat 7 and Cat 8 cables
			Twisted Pair Color Code
				T568A Standard
					Green/white, green, orange/white, blue, blue/white, orange, brown/white, brown
				T568B Standard
					Orange/white, orange, green/white, blue, blue/white, green, brown/white, brown
		### Direct burial
			The practice of burying cables underground
		### Direct burial best practices
			Cables should be STP and have an extra waterproof sheathing
			Burial between six and eight inches underground in protective PVC pipe and placed away from other lines containing electrical current
		### Plenum
			A Teflon-type covering used to cover all types of network cables
			Doesn't release harmful gasses when exposed to extreme heat
			Used in ceilings ,where air is circulated, or tight spaces
		### Optical
			The transmission method that depends on light pulses for data transfer
		### Fiber
			Small strings of flexible glass that are used in optical data transmission
		### Fiber Optic Cables
			A fiber surrounded by a rubberized coating and provides speeds from 100 Mbps to 10 Gbps over a max distance of several miles
			Immune to both electrical interference and wiretapping
					NOTE: There are ways to wiretap fiber
		### Single-mode Fiber
			Carries only one light path
			Typically sourced by a laser
			Very long transmission distance
		### Multimode Fiber
			Carries multiple light paths and is sourced by an LED
			Shorter Transmission distance than single-mode fiber
	## Peripheral Cables
		What are peripheral cables?
			Cables that attach peripheral devices to computing devices
			i.e
			printers, keyboards, monitors, and USB devices
		USB 2.0
			Max speed of 480 Mbps
			Referred to as Hi-speed
		USB 3.0
			Max Speed of 5 Gbps
			Referred to as Super Speed
		Serial Cable#card
			Used for serial communications with a corresponding serial connector on the end
			DB9 is the most common serial connection type and has <ins>nine pins</ins> .
		Thunderbolt
			A combination of PCI Express 2.0 x4 and DisplayPort 1.x tech
			Made primarily for video transmission but can be used for other peripheral devices
			There are 4 standards
				Thunderbolt 1 and 2 terminate in Mini-DisplayPort
				Thunderbolt 3 and 4 terminate in USB-C
	## Video Cables
		Designed to transmit data to visual display units
		### High-definition multimedia interface (HDMI)
			Has 19 pins
			Developed to transmit high frame rates and digital audio on a single cable
		### DisplayPort (DP)
			Developed to use less power than previous video cables
			Backward compatible with VGA and DVI
			Standard cables have two hooks to lock it in place
		### Digital visual interface (DVI)
			Addresses the shortcomings of analog video transmission
			Can transmit digital video signals to display units
			Typically white cables
		### DVI connector standards
			DVI-A: Analog connection only
			DVI-D: Digital connection only
			DVI-I: For both digital and analog signals
		Video graphics array (VGA)
			The oldest video standard still in use today
			Strictly analog
			Typically a blue color
	## Hard Drive Cables
		### Hard Drive Connections
			known as drive interfaces
			either on or off-board
			The interface is based on the hard drives requirements
			Consists of circuitry and a header, or port
		### Serial Advanced Technology Attachment (SATA)
			The most common drive interface
			A flat terminating connector that can only plug in one way
			Data cables have seven pins
			Power cables have 15 pins
		### Sata Speeds
			1.0 = 1.5 Gbps
			2.0 = 3 Gbps
			3.0 = 6 Gbps
			4.0 = 16 Gbps
		### Small Computer System Interface (SCSI)
			Commonly used for storage device connections
			Either ribon or round cables containing 50, 68, or 80 wires
			Up to 16 devices, including the motherboard or SCSI controller card
			Can be sing cable or daisy chained
		### External SATA (eSATA)
			External to the housing
			Standard Cables are for data transmission only and do not provide power
			eSATA that provides power is called Power over eSATA, eSATA+, eSATAp, or eSATA/USB
		### Integrated Drive Electronics (IDE)
			Renamed parallel advanced technology attachment (PATA)
			40-pin flat data cables with a colored strip along one edge to indicate the location of pin 1
			Three separate connectors on PATA cables, one for power and two for drives
	## Adapters
		Converts one type of connection or cable technology to another
	## Connector Types
		Connectors are installed at the terminating points of cables to provide connection to compatible components and peripherals
		depends on the cable
		### RJ11
			Standard for telecommunication network interfaces
			For voice and data equipment connections to a service provider or carrier
			Twisted pair cables with four to six wires for connecting to traditional telephone lines of modems
		### RJ45
			A connector that receives 8 wires
			Twisted pair cable with RJ45 connectors are referred to as Ethernet cables
		### F-type
			used with coax cables and satellite data connetions
		### Straight Tip (ST)
			A bayonet style connector used with fiber optic cables
		### Subscriber connector (SC)
			push/pull-style connector used with fiber optic cables
		### Lucent Connector (LC)
			Push/pull-style connector used with fiber optic cables
			Half the size of an SC, making it suitable for office and data center usage
		### Punchdown Block
			An electrical connection where multiple copper wires can be "punched down" or inserted into a slot
			Provides insulation as well as electrical connection to attached wires
		### Micro USB
			The smallest USB connector type
			contains five pins
			direction dependent
		### MiniUSB
			Second smallest USB connector type
			contains five pins
			direction dependent
		### USB-C
			Most recent USB connector type
			Contains 24 pins
			Oval shape
			Reversible connection
		### Molex
			An older two-piece pin and socket interconnection type used for drive connections
		### Lighting Port
			An eight-pin Apple proprietary connector
			Reversible orientation
		### DB9
			Trapezoid shape
			nine-pin connector
			arranged in two rows of four and five pins
			used for serial connections to network device consoles or management ports
## RAM
  
	### Random access memory (RAM)
		A memory bank for data currently in use by a computer or device
		RAM memory is temporary
		Numerous types of RAM
	### Virtual RAM
		Space on a hard drive that can be allocated when additional memory is requested from an application
		Also known as a swap file or paging file
	### Small Outline Dual Inline Memory Module (SODIMM)
		Commonly found in laptops
		smaller than normal DIMM
		come in 100-, 144-, 20-, 204-, and 260pin configurations
		SODIMM defines the physical form factor of the module
	### Double Data Rate (DDR)

		Doubles the transfer rate of memory
		DDR and DDR2 are legacy and no longer used in modern devices
		DDR2 increases DDR speed by two
	### Double Data Rate 3 (DDR3)
		Faster than DDR2 and has 30% less power consumption
		Comes in 240-pin DIMM and 204-pin SODIMM
	### Double Data Rate 4 (DDR4)
		Faster than DDR3 and has less power consumption
		288-pin DIMM and 260-pin SODIMM
	### Double Data Rate 5 (DDR5)
		Doubles the speed of DDr4 to 6.4 Gbps
		Improved power efficiency at 1.1 volts
		Up to 128 GB modules
	### Error-Correcting Code (ECC) RAM
		Has logic built in to detect and correct single-bit memory errors
		Each byte (eight bits) of memory gets a parity bit set that will allow the logic to detect and correct an error in a single bit of each byte
		Does not correct an error in any byte with more than one bad bit
	### Single-Channel
		Memory transfers data in chunks the same size as the system bus's bandwidth
	### Dual-Channel
		Occurs when the memory controller coordinates two memory banks to work in conjunction with one another as a synchronized set when communicating with the CPU, doubling the size of the data transfer
	### Triple-Channel
		Coordinates three memory modules
	### Quad-Channel
		Coordinates four memory modules
## Storage Devices
  
	### Storage Devices
		Components used to store data and files that a computer system needs to operate
	### Hard Drives
		A non-volatile storage device designed for quick access
		also known as a hard disk drive (HDD) or a conventional drive
		uses a magnetic spinning internal platter to store memory
	### HDD Speeds
		Measured in revolutions per minute (rpm)
		Higher speeds generate more and use more energy
	### HDD rpm
		Four speeds
			5,400 rpm
			7,200 rpm
			10,000 rpm
			1500 rpm
	### Storage Form Factors
		Refers to the physical size of the drive
		2.5": Used in laptops more often
		3.5": Used in desktops and are often HDD
	### Solid State Drive (SSD)
		### SSD Memory
			Flash memory technology that contains no moving parts
			Nonvolatile memory, retains data when power is removed
		### SSD Operation
			Erases data in blocks, rather than at the byte level
			Capable of much higher speeds than HDD and can be in much smaller forms
		### SSD Communication Interfaces
			NVMe
			SATA
			PCIe
		### NVMe
			Non-volatile memory express is an open-source standard used to optimize data transfer and can support speeds up to 3.5 GBps.
		### SSD SATA Connection
			Serial AT attachment interface is the slowest of the SSD interfaces.
		### Form Factors
			#### M.2 Form Factor#card
						22 mm wide and varies in length
				common lengths are 80 mm and 60 mm
				"gumstick memory"
				M.2 drives plug into M.2 slots
			#### Mini-serial ATA (mSATA)
				SSD form factor
				30 mm wide 52-pin connector
				Uses the SATA interface
				Either 30 mm x 50.95 mm or 30 mm x 26.8 mm
	### Drive Configurations
		Storage devices can be configured for high availability so that if one disk in an array of disks fails, data is not lost
		### Redundant Array of Independent/Inexpensive Disks (RAID)
			The most common configuration that provide different levels of protection for disks
		### Drive Striping
			A method of storing part of the data on each drive in an array
		### Drive Mirroring
			Keeps a full copy of a disk on another disk
		### RAID 0
			Offers striping of data only
			No redundancy
			Good performance
		### RAID 1
			Offers mirroring of data only
			Requires more storage space to store full copies of data
		### RAID 5
			Offers striping with parity
			Minimum of three drives
			Ability to calculate missing data and rebuild
		### RAID 10
			Offers Striping and mirroring for full redundancy
			Minimum of four drives
		### Parity
			A bit added to a data block that determines if corruption occurred
			Counts the number of bits in a set and sees if the sets have an even or odd amount of bits
	### Removable Storage
		Storage devices that are external and can be relocated
		### Flash Drives
			A removable drive
			Stores large quantities of data in non-volatile, small, and portable forms
			Includes SD cards, USB flash drives, and optical cards
		### Memory Cards
			Flash memory that can store data in a non-volatile state
			Common form factors include SD, CF, micro-SD, mini-SD, and xD
		### Optical Cards
			Flash storage devices that store data through the use of lasers on spinning discs
			Includes CDs, DVDs, and BDs
## Motherboard
  
	### Motherboards
		Provide the circuitry for all the computers to communicate with each other
		All parts interact with the motherboard
	### Motherboard Form Factors
		### Advanced Technology eXtended (ATX)
			Very common form factor
			12" x 9.6"
			24-pin Molex power connection
			Older ATX has 20-pin Molex power connections
		### Micro-ATX
			A bit smaller than ATX and does not have as much expansion ability
		### Information Technology eXtended (ITX)
			A series of significantly smaller form factor boards that were created by VIA Technologies
		### Mini-ITX
			The largest of the ITX form factors
			6.7" x 6.7"
		### Nano-ITX
			4.7" x 4.7"
		### Pico-ITX
			3.9" x 2.8"
		### Mobile-ITX
			2.4" x 2.4"
	### Motherboard Connector Types
		### PCI
			Peripheral Component Interconnect
			Introduced by intel in the 1990s
			Replaced the older 8and 16-bit expansion slots with a 32-bit slot
		### PCIe
			PCI express sends data in a serial stream at higher speeds than conventional PCI.
			Superseded PCI
		### Power Connectors
			24-pin connection through which a motherboard receives electrical power
		### SATA
			The serial ATA connector is used to connect a hard drive or optical drive to a motherboard
		### eSATA
			External SATA is a connection type that allows for external devices to connect directly to the motherboard using a SATA connection
		### Headers
			A motherboard connection type that allows for external components to connect directly to the motherboard
			E.g.
				Power button
				Lights
				Reset button
				Audio jacks
				USB ports
		### M.2
			M.2 is not technically a connection or bus type but rather a form factor
			M.2 supports SATA, USB and PCIe buses
	### Motherboard Compatibility
		### MOBO Compatibility
			Motherboards only work with compatible components
			Motherboard dependent components include bus architecture, chipsets, expansion slots, memory slots, CPUs, power connectors, non-volatile storage, firmware, CMOS batteries, and front panel connectors and headers
		### CPU Sockets
			A CPU socket is the space on the motherboard designed to hold the CPU that contains a specific pin grid array dependent upon the motherboard
			AMD CPUs must run with specific AMD chipsets
			Intel CPUs must run with specific Intel chipsets
		### Server Motherboard
			Typically capable of housing multiple processors with expanded memory and networking capabilities
			ATX sized and specifically designed to fit within the server chassis
		### Multi-socket MOBO
			More than one CPU Socket
		### Desktop MOBO
			Typically ATX or ITX and typically contains one CPU socket
		### Mobile MOBO
			Can be a standard small form factor motherboard
			More commonly custom designed to fit the needs of the manufacturer and with the CPU soldered onto the motherboard
	### Basic Input/Output System (BIOS)/Unified Extensible Firmware Interface (UEFI) settings
		### Basic Input/Output System (BIOS)
			Provides low-level drivers that allow the operating system to interact with various hardware components
			Can't handle drives over 2TB
			Most devices still use BIOS
		### Unified Extensible Firmware Interface (UEFI)
			An updated booting program from BIOS which connects the firmware of a computer to the operating system
			Faster than BIOS
			Not as widely available as BIOS
			Forgoes the self-test process, which increase speed
			Capable of handling drives larger than 2TB
		### Boot Options
			Boot options let you set the sequence of boot devices
			The boot sequence tells the BIOS where to look to load an operating system
			Proceeds in order of the boot sequence list
			Common boot device is the hard drive
			Other devices are optical and flash drives
		### USB Permissions
			A BIOS setting that can specify the permissions that USB devices have on a device
		### Trusted Platform Module (TPM) Security Features
			A dedicated security co-processor or cyrptoprocessor that can be configured for booting authentication
		### Fan Considerations
			Provides airflow to keep components cool during operation
			Considerations include location and use case
			Is it an exhaust fan or a chip fan?
		### Secure Boot
			Ensures that the operating system and drivers are authorized versions without malicious code before loading them
		### Boot Password
			An admin/supervisor password allows the BIOS settings to be changed
			A user/system password allows minor changes such as time and date or boot options
		### Drive Encryption
			An encryption key that can be used to access an encrypted hard drive
			If set that key must be used for decryption, even if the drive is moved to another computer
		### TPM
			A Trusted Platform Module is a security chip that stores cryptographic keys used by the BIOS or UEFI in booting authentication
		### Hardware Security Module (HSM)
			A security device that can create manage and store encryption keys
## CPU
  
	### CPU Architecture
		The Rules by which a CPU communicates with other components
	### x64/x86 Architecture
		Uses CISC technology that is designed to work with either 64 or 32 bits of data at a time
		64 bits is referred to as x64
		32 bits is referred to as x86
	### Advanced RISC Machine (ARM)
		Uses RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computer) ISA
		Also known as Acorn RISC
	### Single-core
		A part of the CPU that reads and executes instructions
	### Multicore
		Multiple Single-cores on a CPU
		Allows different processes to run at the same time
		Most common CPU configuration
		2 Cores run faster than single core but won't exactly double performance
		Also comes in quad, eight and more core configuraitons
	### Multithreading
		Occurs when a CPU is able to run multiple threads at once
		A thread is the string of instructions that the CPU runs
		Hyper-threading technology (HTT) is a form of simultaneous multithreading marketed by intel
	### Virtualization Support
		Allows the CPU to split resources into virtual environments that can act like a physical computer
		AMD-V is AMD's virtualization
		Virtualization Technology (VT) is Intel's virtualization
## Expansion Cards
  
	### Expansion Cards
		A card that can be attached to a motherboard through an expansion slot
		Expansion cards need to be compatible with the expansion slot
	### Sound Card
		Used to improve the sound capabilities beyond the motherboards sound capabilities
		Musicians, composers, and audiophiles may use theses
	### Video Card
		Control the graphics that are displayed
		May be on board (built into the board) or an add-on card
	### On-board Video Card
		Typically provides enough graphic computing for most users
		Gamers and graphic designers may find these lacking
	### Add-on Video Card
		Has its own graphics processing Unit (GPU)
		Used to improve the graphics capability beyond the boards defaults
	### Capture Card
		Converts video signals to digital signals for saving to a storage device
	### Network Interface Card (NIC)
		A device that provides the capability to connect to a network
		Either an on-board or add-on card
		May also be capable of a wire or wireless connection
## Cooling
  
	### Fans
		Move air through a computer to remove heat
		Positioned in the case to allow air to pass between the inside and outside
		Also used on individual components such as CPU coolers and GPU's
	### Heat Sink
		Devices that conduct heat and increase surface area to a component
		Remove heat more quickly than fans alone
		Sometimes have fans built into them
	### Thermal Paste/Pads
		Conduct heat from components to heatsinks
	### Liquid Cooling
		More effective at transferring heat
		Distilled water is the most common liquid
		Uses radiators, water pumps, water tanks, and cooling blocks
		Either internal or external
## Power Supply
  
	### Power supplies
		convert AC power to the DC power that computer components run on.
		Each power supply has specific features related to capacity and connectors.
	### Input 110-120 VAC vs. 220-240 VAC
		Power available at the wall outlet in North America and other parts of the world is, 110 volts AC 120 volts AC is supplied.
		Other countries supply 220 volts AC 240 volts AC.
		Power supplies are designed to accept either or both of these inputs.
		You may find power supplies that accept only one or the other voltage, but most accept both
			Some have a switch to set for the correct input.
		Others accept either input without having to set a switch.
	### Output 3.3V vs. 5V vs. 12V
		The power supply converts the AC power input to DC power that supplies the computer’s components.
		Most power supplies provide +5V and +12V
		Others may also provide -12V, -5V, and +3.3V
		Each output is called a rail
		You may see more than one connector for a specific output
			For example, you may see two +12V outputs, each with its own connector
	### 20-Pin to 24-Pin Motherboard Adapter
		Most power supplies provide DC output power on a standard 24-pin connector, also called the ATX power connector, which plugs into the motherboard
			The connector is keyed so that it can only plug in one way, ensuring that the proper voltages are supplied to the right pins
				Specific pins are designated to supply +5V, +12V, -12V, and 3.3V.
	### Redundant Power Supply
		A secondary power supply that provides power to a device in case of power failure.
	### Modular Power Supply
		A power supply that is not permanently attached to the device.
		This provides the potential for additional power connections to the device.
	### Wattage/Power Rating
		The total capacity of the power supply
		This must be high enough to meet the total power demands of all components in the computer.
		The number and types of devices or components in any given computer determine what wattage rating is needed for the power supply
		They also determine what types of connectors are needed to ensure that you can connect the power supply to each device.
		The type of case the computer is in also needs to be considered to ensure that the power supply physically fits properly.
		To determine the wattage requirement of the power supply, add all of the wattage ratings of the individual components
		Must Meet or preferably exceed power requirements
		The power supply must physically fit into the case
		It also needs long enough cables from the power supply to reach the components
## Multi-Function Devices/Printers and Settings
	### Properly Unboxing a Device and Setup Location Considerations
		When installing a multi-function device or printer, read the manufacturer’s instructions first.
		The next step is to evaluate the needs of the user, the demands that will be placed on the device, and the most appropriate location for maximized efficiency.
		When unboxing the device, be sure to open the box on a stable flat surface.
		Avoid banging or dropping the device.
		Lift the device out in the proper orientation to avoid tipping the device, which is particularly important with printers that use toner cartridges.
	### Use Appropriate Print Drivers for the OS
		Install the appropriate driver for the OS and the device
		Install any updates for the appropriate drivers.
	#### Printer Control Language (PCL) vs. PostScript
		Two printer communication languages
		#### PostScript
			An older technology and is device independent, which allows for print jobs to result in the same output regardless of the printer used
		#### PCL
			A newer and more common printing language that is device dependent
			The printer itself is responsible for some of the printing data
			This may result in slightly different outputs depending on the printer used
	### Device Connectivity
		A printer’s interface is the hardware and software that a printer uses to connect with devices or a network
		The hardware interface used by a printer is commonly called the port
		A printer can only be used with the correct interface.
	#### Printer USB
		The USB connection is the most common wired connection method for printers
		A USB port will allow for the connection of a physical USB cable to the appropriate device.
	#### Printer Ethernet
		Printers that are designed to work on a network will have an RJ-45 ethernet connector
		They connect to the network with an ethernet cable, just as a computer would
		They are easily shared since they are connected directly to the network.
	#### Wireless Printer
		Wireless connections to printers may be made via Bluetooth, 802.11, or a direct ad hoc connection.
	### Public/Shared Devices
		Devices can be shared by multiple users over wired or wireless connections
		Users can connect directly to a printer or through a print server or cloud printing service
		Small office/home office (SOHO) devices can be made available and shared with anyone on a network
			This is true for devices that are connected directly to the network or connected to a computer
			If a printer is connected to a computer, then the operating system must be configured to make the printer available to users on the network.
	#### Printer Share
		Printer share is the process of providing access to a printer to other computers and devices on a network.
	#### Print Server
		A print server sits on the network and handles printing requests from users
		It can spool (temporarily store) requests for printers that are busy, allow prioritization of print jobs, and delete jobs after they’ve been sent
		Print servers may be separate devices or integrated into the printer.
	### Printer Configuration Settings
		Configuration settings are the settings within a printer that can be optimized to best suit the needs of the users
		Configuration settings can be accessible to all users or restricted to authorized users only.
	#### Printer Duplexing
		Enabling duplex mode causes the printer to print on both sides of the paper.
	#### Page Orientation
		There are two options for orientation: portrait or landscape
		In portrait mode, the top of the page is the shorter dimension
		This is how pages are typically printed
		In landscape mode, the top of the page is the longer dimension
		Spreadsheets and slides are often printed in landscape mode.
	#### Printer Tray Settings
		The tray settings control how the printer manages the individual paper trays in the device.
	#### Print Quality
		Print quality settings control resolution and color
		Resolution is expressed in dots per inch (dpi)
		Higher resolutions provide better quality printouts
		Color settings control how vivid the color output is.
	### Print Security
		Giving access to users over the network raises concerns over data privacy
		Steps must be taken to ensure that users cannot read other users’ print jobs.
	#### Print Server Authentication
		Users can be required to authenticate to print servers
		They typically authenticate at one of two security levels: user or administrator. Users are limited to printing and managing their own print jobs
		This prevents other users from seeing or interfering with other users’ print jobs
		Administrators can manage all print jobs.
	#### Printer Badging
		Badging is a function that can be used for the security of a printer. An attached badge scanner is used to access secure printing functions.
	#### Audit Logs
		Audit logs keep a record of all print jobs along with the originating location of the print jobs for later auditing.
	#### Secured Prints
		Secured prints are printouts that require authentication at the physical printer before a print job commences
		This ensures that the intended recipient of the print must be present to reduce the likelihood of another unauthorized user from grabbing or seeing a sensitive print.
	### Network Scan Services
		Network scan services are hardware and software functions designed to input or scan documents
		Scans taken on a printer or scanning device can be set to record the scanned information in a specified manner.
	#### Scan to email
		Occurs when a printer/scanner is instructed to send the scanned image or document to a specific email address or group of addresses, typically in a PDF file.
	#### SMB
		Scan to folder is the process of sending a scanned document or image to a specified folder
		The protocol used by the printer/scanner to send data to a network folder is secure message block (SMB).
	#### Scan to Cloud Services
		occurs when a printer/scanner is instructed to send a scanned image or document to a specified cloud location or service and is supported by popular cloud services, such as Google Drive, OneDrive, and Dropbox.
	#### Automatic Document Feeder (AFD)
		A scanning device that allows for multiple pages to be scanned automatically through the use of a feeder, eliminating the need to manually change the document being scanned
	#### flatbed scanner
		is a scanning device that requires manual placement of a document to be scanned onto a flat scanning surface with a closable lid.
## Printer Consumables
	Printer consumables are the components of a computer that require replacement
	You must be able to install and replace printer consumables
	These questions will be scenario based
	### Laser
		Laser printers create high-quality printouts using a complex process
		The component that actually “draws” the image is a laser
		It works with other components to transfer that image to the paper
		#### Imaging Parts
			Laser printers have the following specific imaging parts.
			Imaging drum—The laser draws the page image onto the imaging drum.
			Fuser assembly—The fuser assembly applies pressure and heat to bond the toner to the paper.
			Transfer Belt—Used in color laser printers, the transfer belt transfers the page image from the imaging drum to the paper.
			Transfer roller—Used in black-and-white laser printers, the transfer roller transfers the page image from the imaging drum to the paper.
			Pickup rollers—Rubber pickup rollers feed paper from the paper tray through the printer.
			Separation pads—To ensure that only one page is fed at a time, separation pads separate the page being fed from the pages underneath it.
			Duplexing assembly—To print on both sides of the paper, the duplexing assembly will flip the paper over.
		#### Imaging Process
			The imaging process of a laser printer specifies the process the printer follows to produce an image.
			Processing—Before any actual printing is done, the image of the full page is placed into memory.
			Charging—A high negative charge is applied to the imaging drum.
			Exposing—The laser draws the image of the page onto the imaging drum by exposing it to the laser light
				The tightly focused laser light removes the negative charge from the imaging drum (neutralizing it), leaving the rest of the drum negatively charged
			Developing—The negatively charged toner is transferred to the imaging drum
				Since the drum is also negatively charged, the toner will only stick to the areas that have been neutralized by the laser
			Transferring—The transfer roller or transfer belt transfers the toner from the drum to the paper.
			Fusing—A fusing roller uses heat and pressure to bond the toner to the paper.
			Cleaning—Any residual toner is cleaned off of the imaging drum.
		#### Maintenance
			Laser printers tend to be expensive, so regular maintenance must be done to keep them running well for a long time
				Manufacturers make this fairly easy for common maintenance tasks like replacing toner
				Other tasks require more knowledge and skill
			Replace toner—Toner is used for each print job, so it must periodically be replaced
				Toner comes in a toner cartridge that is easily replaced by the user.
			Maintenance kit—Printer manufacturers recommend replacing certain parts periodically
				They provide these parts in maintenance kits
				They may include the fuser assembly, transfer belt, transfer roller, pickup rollers, and/or separate pads
			Calibrate—Calibration is done to keep the page image sharp and accurate
				This may be done on a periodic basis and when parts are replaced
				Each printer will have its own calibration process
			Clean—Toner is a fine powder that will leave residue in the printer
				Keeping the inside of the printer clean will keep it running well and prevent loose toner from showing up on printouts
	### Inkjet
		Inkjet printers create high-quality printouts, though the resolution can be lessthan that of laser printers
		They are also much less expensive
			Several components work together to transfer the page image to the paper
		They create the page image by spraying ink dots onto precise points on the paper
		#### Imaging Parts
			Inkjet printers have the following specific imaging parts.
			Ink cartridge—Ink is stored in ink cartridges that supply ink to the print heads
					There may be a separate cartridge for each of the standard colors used—cyan, magenta, yellow, and black
				Some inkjet printers use a combined color cartridge with cyan, magenta, and yellow ink, plus a black ink cartridge.
			Print head—The print head draws the ink from the cartridge and ejects it onto the paper
				Some printers combine the ink cartridge and the print head into a single unit
			Roller—Paper is fed through the printer by the roller.
			Feeder—Paper may be fed from either a simple paper tray or a feeder
				Most feeders have adjustments to allow you to feed papers of varied sizes
			Duplexing assembly—The duplexing assembly flips the paper over so that you can print on both sides
					It is not found in all inkjet printers.
			Carriage belt—The carriage holds the print heads and gets moved into position so the print heads can eject the ink onto the proper spot on the paper
					The belt moves the carriage into position so the print heads can eject the ink onto the proper spot on the paper
		#### Calibration
			Calibration is done to keep the page image sharp and accurate
			This may be done on a periodic basis and when parts are replaced
			The printer should be calibrated whenever a print head or ink cartridge is replaced
			Each printer will have its own calibration process
		#### Maintenance
			Inkjet printers require minimal maintenance, which is easily done using features available through the printer’s menus or software
				To determine if maintenance is needed, test pages specifically designed to point out flaws can be printed from the printer’s menus or software
			Clean heads—Printer heads can get clogged over time. Cleaning the heads can be done by selecting a menu option or through the printer’s software
					When initiated, the printer will run through a head-cleaning process
			Replace cartridges—When the ink in a cartridge has been used up, the cartridge must be replaced
					A calibration should be done after replacing a cartridge
			Calibrate—Calibration is done to keep the page image sharp and accurate
					This may be done on a periodic basis and when parts are replaced
				The printer should be calibrated whenever a print head or ink cartridge is replaced
				Each printer will have its own calibration process
			Clear jams—Paper may get jammed in the printer as it is fed through
				It may be cleared manually by simply pulling it out
				Some printers provide a way to run just the feeder, either manually by turning a wheel or by running the feed motor
	### Thermal Printers
		Thermal printers use chemically treated thermal paper that changes color when heated
			It is the heat, rather than ink, that transfers the image to the paper
			Thermal printers are simple and inexpensive
			Common uses are for receipts and shipping labels
			The image printed is sensitive to light and heat, so it will fade over time
		#### Imaging Parts
			Thermal printers have the following specific imaging parts.
			Feed assembly—The feed assembly feeds the thermal paper through the printer.
			Heating element—The heating element heats specific areas of the thermal paper as it scrolls by
				The heated areas darken to create the image.
			#### Special Paper and Heat Sensitivity
			Thermal paper is chemically treated paper that changes color when heated
					This makes the print out vulnerable to damage or degradation when exposed to heat
		#### Maintenance
			Maintaining thermal printers is simpler than other types of printers
			There are fewer moving parts and no ink.
			Replace paper—Thermal paper comes in rolls of various sizes
				Replacing the paper is a simple matter of removing the empty roll and inserting the new roll
				There is a button or wheel that can be used to feed the paper into the printer after the new roll is inserted
			Clean heating element—The heating element should be cleaned periodically to ensure proper heat transfer to the paper
					Isopropyl alcohol is typically used as the cleaning agent
				Some printers come with a pen or pad to make it easier to access the heating element for cleaning
			Remove debris—Paper fragments and dust will accumulate in the printer over time
					This debris should be removed as needed
				A commonly used method is to blow out the debris with compressed air
	### Impact
		Impact printers use a matrix of pins to strike an ink ribbon to transfer ink to the paper
			The most common type is the dot-matrix printers
			A unique feature of impact printers, as compared to other printers, is that they can print multiple copies simultaneously using carbonless or no carbon required (NCR) paper.
		#### Imaging Parts
			Impact printers have the following specific imaging parts.
			Print head—The print head is a row or rectangular matrix of metal pins
					The pins that strike the ink ribbon form the character to be printed
			Ribbon—The ribbon holds the ink that is transferred to the paper by the print head during printing.
			Tractor feed—The tractor feed feeds the paper through the printer.
		#### Impact Paper
			Impact paper is simply paper made for impact printers
				It may come on a roll or as fanfold paper in single-ply, duplicate, or triplicate
					Duplicate and triplicate paper transfers the image through to all copies simultaneously
		#### Maintenance
			There is little maintenance required on impact printers
				In most printers, these items can all be replaced by the user.
			Replace ribbon—Ribbon replacement is usually a simple task
					The new ribbon should be taut, leaving no slack.
			Replace print head—The pins in a print head can get bent or broken, so the print head will eventually need to be replaced
					The print head does get hot due to the friction of rapidly moving parts, so care must be taken to ensure it cools down before touching it
				It is held in by a lever that must be released to remove the old print head
			Replace paper—Paper replacement requires a bit more effort than in other types of printers
					The paper has holes along the side that are placed into the tractor feed
				These must be aligned so that the paper feeds properly
				Also, since the paper is continuous, as opposed to separate sheets, the top of the page must be lined up properly
	### 3-D Printer
		A 3D printer is not technically a printer but a fabrication machine through a process called additive manufacturing
		A three-dimensional object is created by adding layers of filament or liquid resin on top of one another to produce a specified output
		#### Filament
			Filament is a plastic substance used by 3D printers to create an object
			A 3D filament printer’s components include the frame, the printing plate or print bed, the extruder, the cooling fan, the printed circuit board (PCB), and the filament
			Filament comes on a spool
			The main three things to consider when choosing a filament type are the size, type, and color
		#### Resin
			Resin is a liquid substance contained in a reservoir used to build a specified object
			The resin is hardened by a UV light layer by layer to create the object to as stereolithography/digital light processing printers (SLA/DLP)
		#### Print Bed
			The print bed is the plate on which the object will be printed
			In a filament printer, the print bed is located at the bottom of the printer with the printer creating the object from the bottom up
			The print bed on a resin printer is often located at the top of the printer with the printer creating the object from the top down, appearing to rise from the printer.
